FILTERED AND SIFTED

ISHAN LEVY
A functor \(f:C \to D\) is ﬁnal if the natural map \(\colim g\circ f \to \colim g\) is an isomorphism for any functor for which it exists.

Lemma 0.1. \(f\) is ﬁnal iﬀ for any \(d \in D\), the category \(d/f\) is nonempty and connected. Moreover, to test ﬁnality it suﬃces to check on corepresentable functors.

Proof. We will show that the category of cones from \(D\)-shaped diagrams is equivalent to those from \(C\)-shaped diagrams via the natural restriction iﬀ \(f\) is ﬁnal.

Suppose that \(d/f\) is nonempty and connected. We can construct an inverse as follows. Given a cone from \(C\) to \(x\), its extension to \(D\) will be deﬁned on \(d \in D\) by choosing some map to an element of \(c\) and then composeing with the map to \(x\). Connectedness of \(d/f\) shows that this is well deﬁned, functorial, and it is easily checked to be an inverse.

Now suppose that \(f\) is ﬁnal. Consider the functor \(D \to \Set \) given by \(\Hom (d,-)\). Its colimit is the set of connected components of \(d/D\), which is a singleton. Its composite with \(f\) is the functor associated to the category of elements of \(d/f\). The colimit of this is the connected components of \(d/f\) so we see it is connected and not empty. □

As an example, for the poset of natural numbers \(\NN \), the inclusion of any inﬁnite subset is ﬁnal.

Deﬁnition 0.2. A category \(J\) is ﬁltered iﬀ for any \(a,b \in J\), there is a map to a common \(c\), and for any maps \(a \to b,c\) there are maps \(b,c \to d\) so that the two composites \(a \to d\) agree.

The natural numbers as a poset is ﬁltered.

Deﬁnition 0.3. A category \(J\) is sifted if for any \(a,b\in J\), the category under \(a,b\) is connected.

Any ﬁltered category is sifted, \(\Delta ^{op}\) is sifted, and the category consisting of two maps \(a \to b\) with a common section is sifted (colimits of the last one are called reﬂexive coequalizers).

Lemma 0.4. \(J\) is sifted iﬀ the inclusions \(\Delta : J \to J^n\) are ﬁnal for all \(n\). It suﬃces to take \(n=2\).

Proof. Since composites and products of ﬁnal functors are ﬁnal, it suﬃces to think about \(n=2\). Then via the characterization of ﬁnal functors, we are done. □

Lemma 0.5. \(J\) is ﬁltered iﬀ the inclusions \(\Delta : J \to J^G\) are ﬁnal for all ﬁnite diagrams \(G\). It suﬃces to take \(G = \cdot \leftarrow \cdot \rightarrow \cdot \) and \(\phi \) or \(G = \cdot \cdot \) and \(\cdot \rightrightarrows \cdot \)

Proof. By induction, for any ﬁnite family of arrows \(f_i\) in \(J\), there are maps\(g_i\) so that the composites \(g_i\circ f_i\) only depend on the codomain. Applying this to all the arrows in some \(F \in J^G\), we see that \(F/\Delta \) is nonempty. Applying this to the arrows in any two natural transformation to a constant functor shows that \(F/\Delta \) is connected.

Conversely, if \(\Delta \) is ﬁnal for \(\cdot \cdot \) and \(\cdot \rightrightarrows \cdot \), one sees from the ﬁrst \(J\) is sifted, implying the ﬁrst condition of ﬁlteredness. The second condition is implied since given \(a \to b, c\), we can ﬁrst ﬁnd a map from \(b,c\) to a common object so WLOG \(b=c\). Then the condition on \(\cdot \rightrightarrows \cdot \) gives the second property of ﬁlteredness. Alternatively, if the condition is satisﬁed for \(\phi \) and \(\cdot \leftarrow \cdot \rightarrow \cdot \), the ﬁrst condition is satisﬁed since \(\phi \) shows that \(J\) is connected. We can induct on the ’distance’ between two objects using the second graph to show that the ﬁrst condition is satisﬁed. The second condition follows immediately from the second graph. □

Lemma 0.6. \(J\) is sifted iﬀ \(J\)-shaped colimits commute with ﬁnite products in \(\Set \).

Proof. The comparison map for the commutativity of ﬁnite products and colimits factors as \[\colim _J{\lim }_I D \to \colim _{J^I}{\lim }_ID'\to{\lim }_I \colim _JD\] Where the ﬁrst map is induced by the diagonal functor, and the second is the comparison map for distributivity of ﬁnite products. The second map is always an isomorphism in \(\Set \), and the ﬁrst is always an isomorphism iﬀ \(J\) is sifted. □

Lemma 0.7. \(J\) is ﬁltered iﬀ \(J\)-shaped colimits commute with ﬁnite limits in \(\Set \).

Proof. By the results on sifted colimits, it suﬃces to show that for a sifted category, commutativity with an empty limit and pullbacks is equivalent to being ﬁltered. First suppose the commutativity is satisﬁed. The commutativity with the empty limit shows that the undercategory for \(a \in J\) is connected. Given a span \(c \leftarrow a \to b\), we can consider the corresponding diagram of corepresentable functors, \(J\times (\cdot \rightarrow \cdot \leftarrow \cdot ) \to \Set \). The commutativity here says that the pullback of the connected components of the under categories of \(a,b,c\) is the same as the connected component of the undercategory for the diagram \(c \leftarrow a \to b\). Now we can induct on the distance between two objects in the undercategory of \(a\) to show that the under category of the span is nonempty.

Conversely, since the undercategory of \(a\) is connected, \(J\) commutes with the empty limit. It is easy to see that equalizers (ﬁnite limits) distribute over ﬁltered colimits in \(\Set \), so that combined with the fact that \(J \to J^I\) is ﬁnal when \(J\) is ﬁltered and \(I\) ﬁnite gives that ﬁltered limits commute with ﬁnite limits. □

There should be more conceptual proofs of these things...

We can now try to understand universal cocompletions of small categories \(C\) with respect to colimits or special types of colimits.

Proposition 0.8. The Yoneda embedding \(C \to \Set ^{C^{op}}\) is the universal cocompletion of \(C\), i.e \(\Hom (C,D) = \Hom _{ccts}(\Set ^{C^{op}},D)\) for any cocomplete \(D\), where the isomorphism is given by left Kan extension along the Yoneda embedding.

Proof. Any morphism of cocontinuous functors in \(\Hom _{ccts}(\Set ^{C^{op}},D)\) is completely determined by the restriction to \(C\) since \(C\) generates \(\Set ^{C^{op}}\) under colimits (via the category of elements/Grothendieck construction). In the other direction, this construction allows us to extend any \(F:C\to D\) to \(\hat{F}: \Set ^{C^{op}} \to D\) by taking the colimit in \(D\) along this construction. Then it suﬃces to observe that such an extension has a right adjoint given by sending \(d\) to the functor \(c \mapsto \Hom (Fc.d)\), so it cocontinuous. One easily checks it is an (absolute) Kan extension. □

Really this statement is \(2\)-categorical, i.e. \(\Hom (C,D) \to \Hom _{ccts}(\Set ^{C^{op}})\) is an equivalence. There is an \(\infty \)-categorical analog, where \(\Space \) replaces \(\Set \).

Deﬁnition 0.9. Given a small category \(C\), \(\Ind (C)\) is the universal cocompletion with respect to ﬁltered colimits, and \(\Sind (C)\) is the universal cocompletion with respect to ﬁltered limits.

Call an object \(c\) compact if \(\Hom (c,-)\) preserves ﬁltered colimits, and call it scompact (which I made up) if \(\Hom (c,-)\) preserves sifted colimits. The following proposition characterizes \(\Sind \) and \(\Ind \) (as well as the Yoneda embedding if you remove tha adjectives).

Proposition 0.10. Suppose that \(C\) has ﬁltered (sifted) colimits, and is generated under ﬁltered (sifted) colimits by a small set of (s)compact objects, which are the full subcategory \(C'\). Then \(C\) is \(\Ind \) or \(\Sind \) of \(C'\).

Proof. Here we will assume ﬁltered. Given a functor \(C \to D\) preserving ﬁltered colimits, it is determined by its behavior on \(C'\). □

Ind is the universal cocompletion with respect to ﬁltered colimits.

Sind is the universal cocompletion with respect to sifted colimits.

Many large categories are Ind of their compact objects

Categories of models of algebraic theories are Sind of the opposite of the Lawvere category.
